Introduction

Mission & Objectives

To gain basic knowledge and skills to perform in a coffee setting and to produce drinks within industry standards. This guide is your daily reference — designed to stay behind the counter and support consistent, high-quality results.

  • Produce latte, cappuccino, and other main drinks as per industry standard.
  • Develop good barista skills, including hygiene awareness, cleaning, and organising.

Origins

History of Coffee

Discovered 9th – 11th Century

Coffee was discovered in the south-western part of Ethiopia around the 10th century. From these ancient origins, coffee has become one of the most traded commodities in the world.

The Legend of Kaldi

According to legend, Kaldi was the Ethiopian goat herder who discovered the coffee plant. Kaldi noticed that his herd of goats became very energetic and restless after eating the red berries from a certain bush. This prompted him to try the berries for himself, but when Kaldi took them to Islamic Sufi monks at a monastery, they disapproved and threw them into the fire. However, the aroma that emerged from the fire was so enticing that the roasted beans were taken from the embers and dissolved in hot water. That was the first cup of coffee.

The Coffee Belt

Coffee grows between the tropics. It needs constant temperature and humidity to grow — conditions that exist in tropical areas. Coffee grows in Central and South America, Africa, Asia, and the northern part of Australia. The main coffee-growing countries are Brazil, Vietnam, and Colombia.

Coffee Species

Arabica vs Robusta

There are currently 130 species of coffee classified. Only two are commonly cultivated: Arabica and Robusta.

Arabica
800 – 2,200 m.a.s.l.
0.8 – 1.4% caffeine
Sweet, acidic, fruity
~60% of world production
Robusta
0 – 800 m.a.s.l.
1 – 4% caffeine
Bitter, nutty, woody
~40% of world production

Robusta grows at low altitudes. It develops a lot of caffeine to protect itself from predators like bugs or fungi, but this also gives a lot of bitterness. Arabica grows at high altitudes where there are fewer predators, making it a safer environment. It is more acidic than Robusta and has less body, but is less bitter because it has less caffeine.

From Farm to Cup

The Coffee Plant & Processing

The Coffee Plant

The coffee plant is part of the Rubiaceae family. It is an evergreen shrub that produces red fruits. The fruits are very sweet and can be attacked by some bugs called Broca.

The Coffee Fruit

Under a thick skin (exocarp) there is a thin layer of fruit pulp (mesocarp). Underneath the fruit pulp there is a thick, white protective layer called parchment (endocarp) and the seed (endosperm).

Processing Methods

The Natural Process

The cherries are left to dry in the sunlight for several days until they reach a moisture content of 10–12%. The farmer will rotate them to ensure even drying and prevent mould formation. This process is very popular in countries like Brazil, Yemen, and Ethiopia — producing coffees with high sweetness, low acidity, high body, and fruity flavours.

The Washed Process

In countries where water is plentiful, the cherries are passed through a pulping machine, sorted by weight, and deposited in a fermentation tank. Naturally occurring enzymes dissolve the pulp until it can be washed from the bean — a process that takes 12 to 72 hours depending on temperature and humidity. Once fermented, the washed coffee is spread out to dry until it reaches around 10–12% moisture content.

The Pulped Natural Process

A depulper removes the skin and pulp from the coffee cherry, and the seeds are dried while mucilage is still covering the parchment. The cup results generally show more cleanliness and uniformity than the conventional natural process. The sugar-rich mucilage naturally increases sweetness and fruitiness.

Core Technique

Espresso

Espresso is a method of coffee extraction that takes finely ground coffee, compacts it into a portafilter, and forces hot water through it under pressure to make a concentrated coffee beverage.

Dose
7–10g single / 14–20g double
Yield
25–35ml single / 50–60ml double
Shot Time
20–30 seconds
(sometimes 25–35 seconds)

Step-by-Step Technique

  1. Remove portafilter from grouphead and flush group

    Clear any old coffee residue and heat the grouphead with fresh water.

  2. Wipe basket clean and dry

    Ensure the basket is free from moisture and leftover grounds.

  3. Dose and distribute the desired grams of coffee

    Use your grinder to dose the correct weight into the basket. Distribute evenly.

  4. Tamp consistently, flat & ergonomically

    Apply firm, level pressure to create a uniform coffee bed. Keep your wrist straight.

  5. Clean loose grounds from portafilter surfaces

    Wipe the rim and spouts clean to ensure a proper seal in the grouphead.

  6. Insert portafilter and start the pump immediately

    Lock in and engage the pump in one continuous motion to avoid burning the puck.

  7. Observe the flow and stop pump appropriately

    Watch the stream colour and consistency. Stop when you reach your target yield.

  8. Serve or use to make espresso-based drinks

    Present your drinks with care — first impressions matter.

  9. Remove portafilter and knock out spent grounds

    Tap the puck into the knock box cleanly.

  10. Wipe basket clean and flush group

    Keep everything fresh for the next extraction.

  11. Return portafilter to group head to keep preheated

    A warm portafilter ensures better thermal stability for your next shot.

Quality Control

Extraction & Troubleshooting

A well-extracted espresso presents aromas of chocolate or nuts and flavours of citrus fruits, stone fruits, black tea, or vanilla. It will always have a perfect balance of sweetness, acidity, and bitterness.

Under-Extracted
Sour, astringent
Very long, unpleasant finish

Balanced acidity, sweetness
Round body, smooth finish
Golden-brown crema
Over-Extracted
Bitter, watery, dry
Short, harsh finish
Dark, burnt crema

Channeling

If coffee is not distributed properly in the portafilter, channeling will appear. Channeling is caused by water flowing unevenly through and around the coffee bed. Any shock applied to the portafilter will create cracks in the coffee bed. Proper distribution, tamping technique, and careful insertion into the grouphead are the main factors in avoiding channeling.

Grinder Troubleshooting

USE A FINER GRIND
or increase brew time
Tastes: Too Strong, Bitter, Dark

Grinder Calibration

Think of it like pouring water through rocks versus sand. When you grind coarse, the extraction time is short because water travels fast through the larger gaps. When you grind fine, the extraction time is longer because the gaps are smaller.

Remember

When adjusting the grinder, always purge for 2–3 seconds to remove the coffee between the blades before pulling your next shot. Coarser grind = shorter extraction. Finer grind = longer extraction.

Milk Technique

Milk Steaming

Key Facts

Always use fresh, cold milk. Pasteurised milk has a shelf life of 8–12 days. Before steaming, milk should be at 3–5°C. The ideal steamed milk temperature is 55–65°C. Never steam above 70°C — the sugars will burn and the protein chains will break.

Step-by-Step Milk Steaming

  1. Purge the steam wand

    Excess condensation can build up in the steam wand. Place a cloth over the end and turn it on for a few seconds to clear it.

  2. Position the steam wand in the jug

    Fill the jug just under halfway with fresh cold milk. Sit the steam wand directly down the spout with the tip just below the surface, about 0.5cm deep, shifted halfway between the centre and the side.

  3. Stretch the milk

    Introduce air into the milk to create foam. The closer the tip of the steam wand is to the surface, the more foam the milk will have.

  4. Work the milk

    Once you have enough foam, submerge the tip of the steam wand into the milk to stop additional air entering. The whirlpool will cause all the bigger bubbles to be sucked into its vortex and create micro-foam.

  5. Heat up the milk

    Once the jug becomes too hot to touch it will be around 60°C — time to turn the steam wand off. At 70°C, milk proteins start denaturing, removing natural sweetness and giving a burnt taste.

  6. Wipe clean the steam wand and purge

    Wipe all residual milk off the steam wand and purge immediately. If left uncleaned, milk can sit inside and dry in the tip.

  7. Polish the milk

    Tap the pitcher on a hard surface and swirl several times until all the bubbles are gone and there is only microfoam. Mix the milk and foam together to pour both at the same time.

  8. Pour the milk

    Pour the milk into the cup. Always present your drinks with a nice latte art pattern on top. First impression matters a lot.

Standards

Safety, Hygiene & Service

Critical Safety

Never touch the steam wand by its metal part — this can cause severe burns. Always touch the steam wand by the protective rubber handgrip. Never touch the rim of the cup or place your fingers inside the cup — this is unhygienic and denotes a lack of professionalism.

Customer Service

  • Friendly Greeting — Welcome customers warmly for a positive and connecting experience.
  • Product Expertise — Understand the menu, including coffee varieties and preparation methods, to assist customers and enhance service.
  • Efficient Service — Prioritise speed during peak hours for prompt order fulfilment and overall customer satisfaction.
  • Adapt to Preferences — Tailor service to customers' preferences, remembering favourites and allowing customisation.
  • Problem Resolution — Develop effective problem-solving skills to address customer concerns, turning potential negatives into positives.

Hygiene Essentials

  • Hand Hygiene — Wash hands regularly, especially before starting work, after handling contaminants, and after using the restroom.
  • Clean Workstations — Regularly sanitise surfaces like countertops, espresso machines, grinders, and utensils. Establish a cleaning schedule using food-safe solutions.
  • Proper Utensil Handling — Keep utensils clean and sanitised to avoid cross-contamination. Implement regular cleaning and replacement of frothing wands and coffee filters.
  • Personal Hygiene — Follow a strict personal hygiene routine, including wearing clean uniforms and using disposable gloves when necessary.
  • Food Safety — Adhere to food safety guidelines for items like milk and pastries. Monitor storage areas for cleanliness, check expiration dates, and discard expired items promptly.

Health & Safety

  • PPE — When working with cleaning solutions or descaling agents, wear gloves, safety goggles, aprons, or uniforms to shield from chemical contact.
  • Chemical Storage — Keep chemicals in assigned areas away from food prep zones. Always check labels for contents, usage instructions, and hazard warnings.
  • Manual Handling — Follow safe manual handling practices when moving or lifting chemical containers. Use proper lifting techniques.
  • Equipment Maintenance — Regularly clean and descale machines using approved chemicals, following the manufacturer's guidelines.
  • Emergency Readiness — Know the location of first aid kits, eyewash stations, and chemical spill cleanup kits. Keep emergency contact information easily accessible.

Maintenance

Water Quality & Cleaning

Water Quality

Water can be soft or hard. The more chlorine, magnesium, calcium, and sodium present, the harder the water becomes. Chlorine is used to disinfect water and can give it an unpleasant smell. You need to fit the espresso machine with a water filter — otherwise hard water can cause scale which can damage your machine.

Backflushing the Espresso Machine

Backflushing is essential to avoid a rancid or dirty taste in the espresso. The machine must be backflushed with detergent at the end of every single day. You can also backflush without detergent several times during service.

  1. Replace the standard filter basket with a blind (no-hole) basket
  2. Use a pinch of backflush detergent in the blind basket
  3. Run the pump 5 times at 15-second intervals with the blind basket in place
  4. Remove the portafilter, run the pump while using a group head brush to remove grime
  5. Rinse the portafilter, place it back, and run the machine 5 times at 15-second intervals again
  6. Rinse both the portafilter and the brewhead thoroughly to remove any remaining detergent
  7. Switch back to your standard filter basket
  8. Cycle through your shot process twice to make sure all detergent has been rinsed out
Important

Make sure you rinse the groupheads properly when using detergent. Residual detergent will taint your espresso.

Cleaning the Portafilters & Baskets

Soak portafilters and baskets in warm, soapy water at the end of each day. Remove any built-up coffee oils that can cause rancid flavours.

Cleaning the Grinder

  • At the end of each day, empty the bean hopper and store beans in an airtight container in a dry, cool place.
  • Wash the bean hopper with warm, soapy water and rinse properly with clean water.
  • If not washed regularly, oils from the coffee beans can build up and give a rancid taste to your drinks.
  • Wipe the grinder with a towel, removing all stains and coffee particles.
  • Clean the blades at least once per week, ideally each day.
  • Replace the blades at least once per year.
